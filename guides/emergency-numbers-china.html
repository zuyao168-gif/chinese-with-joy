Can you dial 911 in China?

No. 911 is a North American number, and mainland China's networks do not treat it as an emergency call — dialing it will not reliably reach any dispatcher. The same goes for Europe's 112: on Chinese mobile networks it typically plays a recorded message (in Chinese) pointing you to 110 or 119, not a live operator. Don't gamble on either.

Memorize the real ones instead — or save this page. Three numbers cover almost everything: 110, 120, 119. They work from any phone with signal, including foreign SIMs and eSIMs, and as of 2026 the calls are free.

Chinese speakers read the digit 1 as yāo in numbers — so 110 is “yāo-yāo-líng” and 120 is “yāo-èr-líng”. Locals will understand yī, but yāo is what you'll hear back.

110 — Police

Theft, assault, a scam turning ugly, a traffic dispute, anything that needs an officer: 110 is China's national police line, and it's the number locals dial first when they're not sure who to call — the dispatcher will route you. Response is generally fast in cities.

The catch is language: dispatchers work in Mandarin. The single most reliable move is handing your phone to any nearby adult with this on screen:

Please call the police for me — dial 110.
请帮我报警，打 110。
Qǐng bāng wǒ bàojǐng, dǎ yāo-yāo-líng.

If something was stolen, you'll want the police report (报警回执) for your insurance and embassy — ask the officers for it before you leave.

120 — Ambulance

Medical emergencies: 120 dispatches an ambulance. Big-city dispatch centers sometimes manage to transfer an English speaker, but you cannot count on it, and in a crisis you shouldn't have to. Two show-phrases do the work:

Please call an ambulance (120) for me.
请帮我打 120 叫救护车。
Qǐng bāng wǒ dǎ yāo-èr-líng, jiào jiùhùchē.
Severe chest pain and trouble breathing — possibly a heart attack or severe allergy. Please call 120 now.
我胸口很痛，喘不过气，可能是心脏病或严重过敏，请马上帮我打 120 叫救护车。
Wǒ xiōngkǒu hěn tòng, chuǎn bú guò qì — qǐng mǎshàng bāng wǒ dǎ yāo-èr-líng.
Chest pain, heavy bleeding, someone unconscious or struggling to breathe: call 120 and stay put. For anything less urgent, a taxi or Didi to the nearest hospital is often faster — every driver knows the way.

119 — Fire (and 122 for traffic accidents)

119 is fire and rescue — fires, but also people trapped in lifts or buildings. 122 is the traffic-accident line; in practice you can also just call 110 for a crash and they'll route it. If you only remember one number for anything involving police, remember 110.

If you don't speak Chinese: show, don't say

Panic plus a language barrier is the worst combination. The strategy that works: get a local to make the call, and let your screen do the talking. These three lines — in Joy's real voice — cover the first thirty seconds of almost any crisis:

I need help.
我需要帮助。
Wǒ xūyào bāngzhù.
Help! (shout)
救命！
Jiùmìng!
I have a serious allergy — this can be life-threatening.
我有严重过敏，可能危及生命。
Wǒ yǒu yánzhòng guòmǐn, kěnéng wēijí shēngmìng.

Emergency call boxes on Chinese streets

Chinese cities are dotted with physical SOS points, and they're worth recognizing: red-and-yellow intercom pillars on metro platforms (press to reach station staff), roadside emergency phones on highways placed at regular intervals, and SOS posts in parks and scenic areas. Pressing one connects you to staff or police for that location — no phone, no SIM, no Mandarin number required.

They're a good fallback if your phone is dead or stolen. Otherwise, a direct call to 110/120/119 — or any shopkeeper making it for you — is faster.

Hospitals and travel insurance

Chinese public hospitals run register-first: before a doctor sees you, you register at the 挂号 (guàhào) window with your passport and pay a small fee, then pay before each step — consultation, tests, medication. Costs are modest by Western standards but vary; mobile payment is standard, and big cities also have international clinics with English-speaking doctors at much higher prices.

I need to go to a hospital.
我需要去医院。
Wǒ xūyào qù yīyuàn.

Keep every receipt — your travel insurer will want them. Check before you fly whether your policy covers China, how claims work, and whether any hospitals offer direct billing; confirm the details with your insurer rather than relying on any list you find online.

Quick answers

Can I call 911 in China?

No. China uses 110 for police, 120 for ambulance and 119 for fire — all free to dial. Dialing 911 on a Chinese network will not reliably reach a dispatcher, so memorize 120 and 110 instead.

What is 110 in China?

110 is China's national police emergency line, staffed around the clock. It's also the safe default when you're not sure which service you need — the dispatcher will route your call. Operators mostly speak Mandarin, so if you can't, hand your phone to a local or show the phrase on this page.

What number do I call for an ambulance in China?

120, free from any phone with signal. Locals read it “yāo-èr-líng” — the digit 1 is pronounced yāo in Chinese numbers. For non-life-threatening problems, a taxi to the nearest hospital is often faster than waiting for an ambulance.

Does 112 work in China?

Don't rely on it. 112 is not China's emergency number — on Chinese mobile networks it typically reaches a recorded message in Chinese directing you to 110 or 119, not a live dispatcher. Use 110, 120 or 119 directly.

What are the emergency call boxes on Chinese streets?

Physical SOS points: intercom pillars on metro platforms that connect to station staff, emergency phones along highways, and SOS posts in parks and scenic areas. Pressing one reaches staff or police for that exact location — useful if your phone is dead or stolen.

Is there an English-speaking emergency line in China?

There is no nationwide English emergency line. Dispatch centers in big cities sometimes have English-capable staff or can transfer you, but availability is never guaranteed. The reliable strategy: have a local make the call, and show the Chinese phrase instead of speaking.

Can a foreigner go to a hospital in China?

Yes. Public hospitals treat foreigners — bring your passport, register at the front window first, and pay as you go at each step. Big cities also have international clinics with English-speaking doctors at higher prices. Keep all receipts for your travel insurer.
